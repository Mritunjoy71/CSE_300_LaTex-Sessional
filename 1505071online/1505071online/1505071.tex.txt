\section{Time-dependent equation}
The form of the Schrödinger equation depends on the physical situation (see below for special cases). The most general form is the time-dependent Schrödinger equation (TDSE), which gives a description of a system evolving with time:
\end{flushleft}
\begin{equation}
   i\hbar \frac{\delta}{\delta t} | \psi (r,t)>=\^{\textbf{H}}|\psi(r,t)>
   \label{eq:1}
\end{equation}
where i is the imaginary unit,$ \hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant which is: $\hbar=\frac{h}{2 \pi}$ , the symbol $\frac{\delta}{\delta t}$ indicates a partial derivative with respect to time t,$ \psi $(the Greek letter psi) is the wave function of the quantum system, r and t are the position vector and time respectively, and $ \^{\textbf{H}} $ is the Hamiltonian operator (which characterises the total energy of the system under consideration).
\\
The most famous example is the nonrelativistic Schrödinger equation for a single particle moving in an electric field (but not a magnetic field; see the Pauli equation).
